Project Overview

This project demonstrates a complete medical device design and development lifecycle. I led the discovery, design, development, and deployment of an improved cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) management system. The project showcases proficiency in design controls, risk management, prototyping, verification testing, and the ability to translate clinical needs into manufacturable medical devices.

🎯 Clinical Challenge

Existing CSF management methods present challenges in accuracy, user ergonomics, and reliability. The goal was to design a device that improves safety, usability, and clinical efficacy while maintaining manufacturing feasibility.

Design Control Process

Following FDA 21 CFR Part 11 design control guidelines, this project implemented a rigorous V-cycle methodology:

1
Design Input
User needs analysis, clinical requirements, regulatory constraints, and design specifications

4
Verification Testing

Design Requirements & Specifications

The design input phase established clear user needs and technical requirements:

Key Requirements

  • Accuracy: ±2mm precision in fluid delivery
  • Ergonomics: Intuitive single-handed operation
  • Biocompatibility: ISO 10993 compliance for contact materials
  • Manufacturability: Cost-effective production with scalability
  • Regulatory: 510(k) pathway compliance

Risk Management & FMEA

Comprehensive Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) identified potential failure modes and mitigation strategies:

Failure Mode Effect Severity Mitigation Strategy
Fluid leakage at connections CSF loss, infection risk High Luer-lock connectors, pressure testing, validation protocol
Calibration drift Inaccurate dosing High Mechanical stops, design for precision, periodic verification
Material degradation Device failure, biocompatibility issues Medium Material stability testing, shelf-life study, sterilization validation
User error in assembly Incorrect operation Medium Design for assembly (DFA), labeling, user training documentation
Mechanical jamming Device malfunction Low Tolerance design, lubrication study, wear testing

Prototyping & Iterative Design

Multiple design iterations were conducted to optimize form, fit, and function:

Phase 1

Concept Design

Initial CAD models and concept validation. Evaluated 3 design concepts against user needs and manufacturability.

Phase 2

Prototype Development

Fabricated first prototype using 3D printing and CNC machining. Conducted design FMEA and preliminary validation testing.

Phase 3

Design Refinement

Addressed FMEA findings and user feedback. Iterated on ergonomics, precision, and assembly design. Finalized material selection.

Phase 4

Verification Testing

Conducted performance validation, fatigue testing, sterilization validation, and biocompatibility testing.

Lessons Learned & Takeaways

User-centered design matters: Early user needs analysis and iterative feedback loops prevented costly design changes later in development.

Risk management is proactive, not reactive: FMEA identified issues before manufacturing, reducing time and cost to remedy.

Design for manufacturability saves time: Considering manufacturing constraints during design phase enabled smooth transition to production.

Documentation is a design tool: Rigorous design history file documentation ensured traceability and facilitated regulatory submission.
